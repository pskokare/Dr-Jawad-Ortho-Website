Why Choosing the Right Knee Replacement Surgeon Matters

Knee replacement surgery, also called knee arthroplasty, is usually advised when the knee joint is badly damaged due to osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, post-traumatic arthritis, deformity, cartilage loss, or long-standing joint wear and tear. When Is Knee Replacement Surgery Needed?

Knee replacement is not the first treatment for knee pain. In many cases, the doctor may first recommend medicines, weight management, knee exercises, physiotherapy, activity modification, braces, or injections. Surgery is usually considered when conservative treatments do not provide enough relief and the patient's daily life is seriously affected.

You may need evaluation for knee replacement if you have:

  • Severe knee pain while walking or standing
  • Pain even at rest or during sleep
  • Difficulty climbing stairs
  • Stiffness that limits knee movement
  • Bow legs or visible knee deformity
  • Swelling that keeps returning
  • Reduced walking distance
  • Advanced osteoarthritis seen on X-ray
  • Dependence on painkillers for daily activity
  • Poor quality of life due to knee pain

A knee replacement surgeon will assess your symptoms, X-rays, medical history, walking pattern, joint movement, and overall health before recommending surgery.

Types of Knee Replacement Surgery

Not every patient needs the same type of knee replacement. The procedure depends on the level of joint damage, age, lifestyle, bone quality, ligament condition, and the part of the knee affected.

1. Total Knee Replacement

In total knee replacement surgery, the damaged surfaces of the knee joint are replaced with artificial components. This is usually recommended when arthritis affects the entire knee joint and causes severe pain, stiffness, and deformity.

2. Partial Knee Replacement

In some patients, only one part of the knee is damaged. In such cases, partial knee replacement may be considered. This procedure preserves more of the natural knee structure and may support faster recovery in suitable patients. However, it is only advised after careful clinical and radiological evaluation.

3. Robotic Knee Replacement

Robotic knee replacement uses advanced robotic assistance to support surgical planning and precise implant placement. The technology helps the surgeon create a personalized surgical plan based on the patient's knee anatomy. It does not replace the surgeon. Instead, it assists the surgeon in improving accuracy during the procedure.

4. Computer-Navigated Knee Replacement

Computer navigation helps the orthopedic surgeon align the knee implant with better precision. This can be useful in complex knee deformities, difficult anatomy, or advanced arthritis cases where accurate alignment is important.

What to Expect Before Knee Replacement Surgery

Before knee replacement, the orthopedic team usually performs a complete assessment. This may include:

  • Physical examination of the knee
  • X-rays and imaging
  • Blood tests
  • Medical fitness evaluation
  • Review of diabetes, blood pressure, heart health, and other conditions
  • Medication review
  • Implant and surgery planning
  • Physiotherapy guidance
  • Counselling about recovery expectations

Patients are also advised to prepare their home for recovery. This may include arranging support for walking, keeping frequently used items within reach, reducing fall risks, and following the doctor's instructions about medicines and food before surgery.

Recovery After Knee Replacement Surgery

Recovery after knee replacement varies from patient to patient. Some people start walking with support soon after surgery under medical supervision. Physiotherapy plays a major role in improving knee movement, strengthening muscles, and helping the patient regain confidence.

Recovery depends on:

  • Age and fitness level
  • Severity of arthritis before surgery
  • Type of surgery performed
  • Muscle strength
  • Weight and lifestyle
  • Diabetes or other medical conditions
  • Physiotherapy compliance
  • Post-surgery wound care

Most patients are guided through a structured recovery plan that includes walking support, knee bending exercises, pain control, swelling management, and follow-up visits. Complete recovery can take time, and patients should follow the surgeon's instructions carefully.

Risks and Safety Considerations

Knee replacement is a commonly performed orthopedic procedure, but like any major surgery, it carries risks. These may include infection, blood clots, stiffness, bleeding, implant-related issues, nerve injury, persistent pain, or the need for revision surgery in some cases.

Patients should immediately contact their doctor if they notice warning signs such as:

  • Sudden chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Increasing calf pain
  • Unusual swelling
  • Redness around the wound
  • Fever
  • Fluid discharge from the surgical site
  • Severe pain that does not improve

A responsible orthopedic surgeon will explain the risks, expected benefits, alternatives, and recovery process before surgery. This is especially important for patients with diabetes, heart disease, obesity, kidney disease, or other medical conditions.

2. When should I consider knee replacement surgery?

You may consider knee replacement if you have severe knee arthritis, constant pain, stiffness, deformity, difficulty walking, or poor quality of life despite medicines, physiotherapy, injections, or lifestyle changes.

3. Is robotic knee replacement better than traditional knee replacement?

Robotic knee replacement can help the surgeon plan and place the implant with greater precision. However, it may not be required for every patient. The best option depends on your knee condition, bone structure, arthritis stage, and surgeon's recommendation.

4. How long does recovery take after knee replacement?

Recovery varies for every patient. Many patients start walking with support soon after surgery, but full recovery and strength improvement take time. Physiotherapy, wound care, and regular follow-up are important for better recovery.

5. Is knee replacement surgery safe?

Knee replacement is commonly performed, but it is still a major surgery. It has possible risks such as infection, blood clots, stiffness, pain, or implant-related issues. A proper medical evaluation and experienced surgical care help reduce risks.

6. Can both knees be replaced at the same time?

In selected patients, both knees may be replaced during one surgical admission. However, this depends on age, fitness, medical condition, severity of arthritis, and the surgeon's assessment.

7. Will I be able to walk normally after knee replacement?

Many patients experience improved walking ability and reduced pain after successful knee replacement and rehabilitation. Results vary depending on the patient's health, surgery type, physiotherapy, and recovery discipline.
